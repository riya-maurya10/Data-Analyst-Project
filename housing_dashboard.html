Scene 2 — Price Distribution
The majority of homes fall in the $250K–$400K range, which together account for over 6,000 listings. The most populated bin is $300K with 2,188 homes. As prices rise, volume drops — only 1,042 homes exceeded $600K. Interestingly, years-since-renovation remains consistently low (0–1 years) across all price bands, suggesting renovation recency isn't strongly correlated with price tier.

Scene 3 — Renovation Patterns
Renovation rates increase sharply with house age. Homes aged 0–30 years show near-zero renovation rates, while homes aged 60–100 years have renovation rates of 10–20%. The 60–69 year age group shows the highest renovation activity. Older homes are being upgraded — signaling investment value in older stock.

Scene 4 — Features by House Age
The top 5 most common house ages (4, 12, 13, 14, 15 years) reveal structural patterns. 4-year-old homes have the highest average bedroom count, reflecting modern family-sized builds. As houses age (12–15 years), bedroom/bathroom counts slightly decrease but remain consistent around 3–4 bedrooms. Floor counts stay between 1–2 across all age groups.

Scene 5 — Key Takeaways
1. Price Volume: Most activity occurs in the $250K–$400K range; the market thins sharply above $500K.

2. Renovation Value: Older homes (60+ years) are being renovated at higher rates — indicating market interest in aged properties with upgrade potential.

3. Modern Builds Dominate: 4-year-old homes are the single most common house age, reflecting a recent construction boom.

4. Stable Features: Bedroom and bathroom counts are remarkably consistent across age groups — suggesting buyer preferences drive construction standards.
